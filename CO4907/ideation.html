Summary

By now you should have identified the problem space you are attempting to explore in this module. You will start to generate solutions to the problem - this is the process known as divergent thinking. You will begin to narrow down these solutions, ready for prototyping. By the end of the session, you will have decided upon where your focus will be - convergent thinking.

Aim

This session aims to further your knowledge and understanding of the ideation process. You will begin to realise your problem space and begin to ideate possible solutions. By understanding your users in more depth, you start to appreciate what issues your users have with a particular problem. So far you have been thinking about problems and solutions using texts, by the end of the session you will have visualised solutions ready for building on the creative process next week.

Activity 2. Deciding

10-35 minutes

Making decisions is always a difficult task whether you are deciding on your own or as part of a team. The methods you will adopt differ depending on your circumstances. Deciding in groups is much easier than on your own. When you are on your own you need to remove bias from the ideas, they are all your ideas and you need to focus on what brings the most to solve the problem within your constraints.

Independently (on your own)

  • Decision matrix

Group (team or stakeholders)

  • Dot voting

If you are deciding on your own you should attempt to elicit as much external feedback as possible through friends/family, stakeholders and end users.

A decision matrix is made up of two axes that you would like to measure your question/idea against. These could be:

  1. Feasibility

  2. Cost

  3. Novelty

  4. Time

  5. Etc…

Essentially these can be anything you want to measure your ideas against.

  • Using the decision matrix (copy and paste it into your space), decide on two measures to measure your HMW questions against. For example, Feasibility and Time - you would have high/low feasibility and short/long term

  • Plot your HMW questions on this decision matrix. This will help you prioritise which problem (question) to start with

    • For example, for each question ask yourself what is the feasibility of solving the question in this module's assignment and how long will it take to solve

    Once you have mapped all your HMW questions onto the decision matrix, you should have all HMW in some/all of the quadrants.

    Depending on your measurement criteria you are looking for the HMW questions that fall into the shorter term and high feasibility as this means you can complete the work within a good time frame and you have the skills to do so. If you are stuck with this activity speak to your tutor.

  • See the example for the How, Now, Wow Matrix in the tutoring space. As you will see you mark up the matrix with How, Now, and Wow. The questions you should be focussing on are contained in the 'Now'. However, you should keep a close eye on the 'How' as these need a little more context. The 'Wow' is probably a little over-ambitious for the timescale you have. Finally, the questions located in the empty quadrant are probably not big/complex enough for a Sprint or not worth solving right now.

  • Depending on your vision, typically you are looking for ones in the ‘Now’ quadrant because these are the easy wins (low risk, high gain)

  • The ‘Wow’ needs more data – it might be too soon for your customers and your business to manage it (it is innovative, breakthrough)

  • How’ requires more exploration due to its complexity (green idea for tomorrow)

If you are working as a team, you could use the dot voting technique.

Dot Voting

  • Dot Voting works best when you are working as part of a group.

  • Like the name suggests you are voting with dots (sticky dots to be precise).

  • Provide your group with dots and ask people to place dots on where they think you should focus your efforts. This provides a heat map of activity.

Activity 3. Understanding your users

35-65 minutes

Before we start to solve the problem, we need to empathise with the user/customer. One of the ways we can do this is by mapping out the user's environment and demographics. You are following this template to start building your persona.

Remember personas are meaningful when they are data-driven. You create the persona based on data to inform your decisions. As these are responsive to data, there is a strong possibility that the persona will change over time, as the data changes.

How to create data-driven personas

  • Title this stage as Understanding Users

  • Using the 'Understanding your customer' template in the Tutor notes section of Miro, copy and paste the template into your space

  • Overlay post-it notes onto the template by answering the questions

    • Start with the macro environment (negative and positive forces) - these are broader conditions outside of your control, examples include; economic, political, socio-cultural, technological, and ecological more information

    • Move on to defining the demographic (who is this type of user?)

      • What headaches fears, hopes and opportunities exist for this demographic?

      • What is the need of this persona?

    • Define this persona with a name and role

  • Complete this activity for each user of your problem (you are expected to have at least two personas for the coursework submission)

Activity 4. Brain dump

75-85 minutes

The brain dump helps you consolidate your thinking into one space. At this point, you should not be adding new information/insights/ideas. You are simply pulling all your thinking together.

  • Using the 'Brain dump' template, complete the sections with specific details

  • Bring together all your input so far and start to frame the problem and think about why you want to solve this problem - use your mind map, persona, HMW, reframed problem

    • Rewrite your problem statement

    • Note the defined HWM question

    • Review the persona

    • Provide details on what the problem is and how you might start to solve this problem

You may feel like you are repeating yourself, but what you are doing is auditing your thinking. Consolidating your insights into a manageable scope.

 

Activity 5. Mind Mapping

85-115 minutes

Mind maps are visual diagrams used to represent words, ideas, tasks etc. They are arranged around a central problem. Colours, drawings, and brief notes are used to enrich the visualisation.

In the centre of this section add the problem you are looking to solve, add children to this node and draw connections between them (these child notes relate to the core problem), and drill down on the child nodes to expand the idea/solution. Create sub-branches for ideas related to the main branch. Select a template of a mind map from the container below this board, and move it into this area to populate, based on your problem statement.

  • From the 'Now' quadrant take a copy of all the Post-it notes and create a mind map for each HMW question

  • Start with the HMW question in the middle and build what you already know about the question, what you need to find out, what form a solution might look like etc. There are no rules as to what your mind map should include - you are trying to build a bigger picture of the problem

    Miro has a Mind Map feature you can use to make it easier to map out. If you use the sidebar you will see two arrows pointing right > click that and select 'Mind Map'

Activity 6. Crazy-8s

0-15 minutes

Crazy-8s are a core component of the Sprint process. It is a rapid sketching exercise that challenges you to sketch eight distinct ideas in eight minutes. This method uses divergent thinking to broaden thoughts about how you might start to solve your problem.

  • Remember (distinct ideas)

  • Rough sketch (be quick)

  • No need for perfection

  • 8 minutes - 1 minute per idea! be strict

  • Fold a piece of A4 paper in half and in half again to have 8 equal segments

  • Crazy-8’s all about fast sketching. Where you will sketch 8 distinct ideas in 8 minutes

  • These ideas should be taken from your Braindump activity

  • The goal is to push beyond your first idea to generate a wide variety of solutions to your problem (divergent thinking)

     

Activity 7. Decide

15-30 minutes

Now you have eight distinct sketched ideas it's time to review each idea holistically and make decisions on which idea (collection of ideas) should be the focus of this Sprint.

For this, we will use the ‘Dot voting’ method, where you can use sticky dots (or draw a dot) to make decisions on what elements of each idea you think are most appropriate in solving your problem.

You might want to involve your network in making these decisions – the more dots the better as this will produce a heat map of what looks promising. You can discuss your sketches with your cohort and tutors to elicit feedback.

Decide which ideas generated the most feedback

If dots were scattered around the Crazy-8, extract elements of the idea that is useful to consider (you can even cut out the idea/element from the paper)

  • Capture the sketch and upload it to your Miro space

  • Present your sketches to your tutor and cohort to elicit feedback, you should present the sketches as they are and not add additional insights

  • Ask the participant to use the dots to vote on what ideas they like most and/or what elements of ideas they like (dots have been prepared in the tutoring space, copy and paste a set into your space)

  • Collect at least 5 sets of feedback

  • Use the dots to make a final decision

     

Activity 8. SCAMPER

30-60 minutes

Focussing on one of the Crazy-8 solutions, you are now going to perform SCRAMPER.

Substitute: What can I substitute to make an improvement? Combine: What ideas, features, processes, or components can I combine? Adapt: What processes, features, or components should I adapt? Modify: What can I make larger or make smaller? Put to another use: What else can it be used for? Who else could use it? Eliminate: What would happen if I removed a feature or part of it? Reverse/rearrange: How can we rearrange the current status for an improved solution? What would happen by reversing the process?.

  • Make a decision on which of the Crazy-8s you wish to explore further (Hint!, use the How, Now, Wow matrix)

  • Using the SCAMPER template (copy and paste into your areas)

  • With one of the ideas, populate the sections, by answering the questions above. For example in the first box, you are to substitute an element of your Crazy-8, you should give this substitution a title, description and a sketch. You can sketch within Miro or on paper and upload the photo.

  • Perform SCAMPER for another idea within your Crazy-8
